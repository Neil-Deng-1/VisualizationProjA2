Write Up

A brief introduction to your dataset, background and your chosen variable(s) or subset.

  • This data set is the “National Statistics by Parent Income Percentile, Gender, and Race” from the Social Mobility Insights category of assigned databases. The dataset is 100 rows, one for each percentile of parent income (e.g 27th percentile is the bottom 27% of the distribution for parents ranked by income). Each row is composed of many columns of the outcomes of their children, born in the years 1978-83. The outcomes are then separated by race and gender.

Rationale for your design decisions. List your implemented interactions. Explain: How did you choose your particular visual encodings and interaction techniques? What alternatives did you consider and how did you arrive at your ultimate choices?
  • The tools and the design of our data visualization were thoughtfully inspired by the lessons learned in our class’s lectures about visual encodings, user interaction, user perception, and more.
  • Describing the tools, the slider on the top allows the user to view which percentile (1-99) of parent earners, and the drop down menu allows the user to select the type of child outcome.
  • For example, one can set the slider to 95% to query only parent earners in the 95th percentile, then the user can select the option for “Percentage of children with high school degree or GED by race and gender” to see just that. The user can then re-adjust the slider of percentiles to see how these rates change as parent income level changes.
  • Through our visualization, one can observe the hidden stories told by the dataset. For instance, male children, both black and white, have been more incarcerated, fail to get a high school diploma or GED, and not attend college than their female counterparts at all income levels of their parents.
  • According to our lecture of “Task and Interaction”, both the percentile-slider tool can and the dropdown menu be both classified as the interaction techniques of dynamic filtering and querying (requirement 1) since they allow the user to witness the outcomes of the children (dropdown menu) based on the parents’ income level (slider). The user can develop a thorough understanding of the entire dataset by viewing it from these many perspectives, contexts, and permutations of the variables.
  • Our bar chart has its x-axis categories as the children’s race and genders, and its y-axis heights as the respective percentages of the children who fulfill the selected criteria. This choice of visual encoding is an example of the technique of reconfiguration, namely from the original, overwhelming table of raw data, to a cleaner succinct bar chart. Now, by observing the heights of each bar, one can easily compare the effects of parental income on children by gender and race.
  • Our data visualization also employs the concept of data elaboration (requirement 2). When a user hovers over a bar, it becomes distinguished through an emboldened outline. Additionally, a tooltip appears, specifying the exact percentage value of the selected demographic and observed outcome.
